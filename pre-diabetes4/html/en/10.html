Everyone Eats Different

What you choose to eat will be different than what other people eat, and that’s okay as long as you follow MyPlate guidelines. You may be a vegetarian, vegan or follow a Mediterranean diet. Everyone’s style of eating is commonly referred to as an eating pattern. Tap on the eating pattern to learn more about it and how it can affect your health:

A vegetarian diet mainly consists of plant-based foods. There are vegetarians that include dairy in their diet (lacto vegetarian) and others that include eggs (ovo vegetarian), or both dairy and eggs (lacto-ovo vegetarian). There are some that may even include a small amount of animal protein in their diet. If they eat mainly plant-based foods but with a little chicken they call themselves pollo-vegetarian. Pesco vegetarians will eat a little bit of fish in their diet. Studies have shown that vegetarian diets may be better at helping people with type 2 diabetes control blood sugar and cholesterol than low-fat diets.
Vegans do not consume any animal products and eat only plant-based foods like fruits, vegetables, grains, and tofu. A 2015 study showed that vegan diets can improve blood sugar and cholesterol levels in people with type 2 diabetes more than low-fat diets.
Mediterranean-type diets are associated with reductions in HbA1c levels, normal blood sugar readings, and delayed need for diabetes medication. is not a specific plan of food, but has general features:
  • plant foods as the main part of diet: vegetables, fruits, whole grains, nuts, and legumes (like beans, peas, and lentils) that are not overly processed
  • olive oil as the main source of fat
  • low to moderate amounts of cheese and yogurt
  • fish and poultry as the main source of animal protein; minimal amounts of red meat
  • fresh fruit with meals instead of desserts
The Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) is a diet consisting of fruits, vegetables, and low-fat dairy foods, and with reduced sodium, saturated fat, and total fat. Studies done with the DASH have shown reductions in blood pressure in people with Type 2 diabetes.

The bottom line is that whichever eating pattern you have, your meals should be spread out throughout the day to avoid overeating and causing a spike in your blood sugar.
